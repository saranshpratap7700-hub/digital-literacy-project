Stay Safe Online' Checklist for Students
UPI PIN Rule: Never enter your UPI PIN to receive money. A PIN is only for outgoing transactions.
QR Code Caution: Do not scan QR codes sent by strangers on WhatsApp or Telegram for "rewards" or "refunds."
Two-Factor Authentication (2FA): Enable 2FA on your university email, WhatsApp, and Instagram accounts to prevent hijacking.
Public Wi-Fi Hygiene: Avoid logging into bank accounts or student portals while connected to free public Wi-Fi (e.g., at cafes or railway stations).
Official App Sources: Only download apps from the official Play Store or App Store; avoid "Modded" APKs for games or premium services.
Privacy Settings: Set your social media profiles to "Private" to prevent scammers from harvesting your personal details for identity theft.
Phishing Awareness: Do not click on links in SMS messages claiming your "Electricity bill is unpaid" or "KYC is expired."
Secure Passwords: Use a unique password for your university portal that is different from your social media and banking passwords.

Research Reflection (100–150 words):
What surprised me most during this research was the psychological manipulation used in UPI fraud. I always assumed cybercrime was purely technical—like hacking or coding—but many scams rely on "social engineering," creating a false sense of urgency or excitement that causes even tech-savvy students to ignore red flags. The fact that scammers can trick someone into voluntarily entering their PIN by simply reversing the logic of a transaction is a sobering reminder of how vulnerable we are.
As a result, one habit I will personally change is my approach to permissions and links. I often reflexively click "Allow" on app permissions or click links sent in group chats. From now on, I will adopt a "Zero Trust" policy: verifying the source of every link and double-checking the "Request" vs. "Pay" labels on every UPI notification before interacting with them.
